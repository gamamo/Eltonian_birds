### Abstract: 
- Seed-dispersal interactions are important for maintaining and recovering all ecosystems. Recent decades have seen an exponential interest in studying these interactions, especially those between birds and plants, using ecological networks as key tools for addressing long-standing ecological questions. However, the use of seed-dispersal networks may still represent limited information on this interaction with potential geographical and taxonomic biases. Here, we evaluated the knowledge gap on seed-dispersal networks between birds and plants to quantify the size of our ignorance, describing the Eltonian shortfall for this important interaction. We compiled 538 bird-plant seed-dispersal networks across all biogeographic realms and evaluated their geographic and taxonomic coverage. We found that sampling coverage and the number of interactions are largely skewed towards the Neotropics, Oriental and Palearctic realms, and that biogeographic realms in Africa and Oceania have fewer recorded networks. Over 23% of all potential seed-dispersing birds are present in the networks, but this taxonomic gap is larger in Australian, Oceanian and Madagascan realms, where only ~10% of the species from the biogeographic realm pool of bird seed dispersers have been recorded. By highlighting key knowledge gaps and explicitly quantifying the size of the Eltonian shortfall, we also provide future directions for more targeted data collection and also for species identification.
